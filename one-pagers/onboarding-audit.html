Onboarding · psychometric rewrite

Read users in 60 seconds, don't interview them for ten minutes.

Lens · Behavioral science Status · v2 audit · supersedes v1 Surface · Mobile onboarding

01 The reframe

Onboarding is not a form. It's a 60-second psychometric instrument disguised as a vibe. The goal isn't to collect facts — facts are the easy part. The goal is to infer the user's psychological profile from as few inputs as possible, then mirror it back so they feel seen. Travel personalization that works runs on six dimensions; everything else is plumbing.

Reframe · 01

Forced choice beats Likert

Asking "rate your sensation seeking 1–7" returns noise. Asking "pick your perfect Saturday" between four equally attractive images returns signal.

Reframe · 02

Behavior beats attitude

What people say they want diverges from what they book. Anchor questions on past behavior — "last big trip you took" — not future hypotheticals.

Reframe · 03

Identity beats demographics

"I'm the kind of traveler who…" is a self-perception nudge that improves retention. Age, gender, occupation are weakly predictive and high-friction.

02 The six dimensions that predict satisfaction

From travel-motivation literature (Pearce's Travel Career Pattern, Plog's allocentric–psychocentric continuum, Yoo & Gretzel), positive-psychology motives (HEMA), and general personality science (Big-Five short forms, BSSS-4 sensation seeking, maximizer–satisficer). ACTZ doesn't need to measure all of Big-Five; it needs the six axes that map onto activity selection, itinerary structure, and group dynamics.

01

Stimulation appetite

How much novelty and arousal a person seeks. The single most predictive trait for adventure-travel apps. Stable across life, mostly genetic, but expression shifts with age.

Familiar · grounded Novel · intense
Instruments: BSSS-4 · Plog allocentric–psychocentric
Powers: activity intensity ranking, difficulty filter, surprise tolerance
02

Cognitive style · planning

Maximizers want every option compared; satisficers want a good-enough plan now. Mismatched UI causes either anxiety or boredom. Strongly predicts whether AI itineraries feel magical or threatening.

Satisficer · go with the flow Maximizer · weigh every option
Instruments: Schwartz Maximization Scale · Need for Cognition
Powers: AI itinerary density, "draft for me" vs "show options"
03

Social orientation

Energy source on a trip. Extraverts charge in crowds; introverts recover in solitude. Predicts crew vs solo, willingness to meet strangers, decision style, group-size comfort.

Solitary · recharge alone Communal · charge in crowds
Instruments: TIPI Extraversion · attachment-style proxies
Powers: crew vs solo, crew-finder opt-in, group decision UX
04

Hedonic vs eudaimonic

Pleasure-seeking ("I want to feel great") vs meaning-seeking ("I want this to matter"). Travel-motivation research finds these are independent — most people are mixed, but the ratio shapes everything.

Hedonic · joy, sensation Eudaimonic · growth, meaning
Instruments: HEMA scale (3 items each) · VIA curiosity strength
Powers: activity tone (party vs reflective), curation copy register
05

Risk tolerance

Two flavours: physical (will you raft Class IV?) and financial (will you spend $1200 on one dinner?). Self-report is unreliable — measure via revealed preference between calibrated trip options.

Cautious · proven · safe Bold · stretch · exposed
Instruments: DOSPERT short · revealed-choice anchor pairs
Powers: safety filter, upsell ceiling, insurance defaults
06

Identity & commitment

Self-perception theory (Bem): when a person names what kind of traveler they are, later behavior bends to match. Identity statements are the lowest-friction, highest-retention question in onboarding — they cost a second and pay for years.

Tourist · trip is a one-off Traveler · trip is identity
Instruments: Bem self-perception cue · TCP traveler-career stage
Powers: long-term retention, referral propensity

03 Six rules for asking

The principles below come from survey methodology, behavioral economics, and a decade of mobile-onboarding A/B tests across consumer apps. Each cuts measurement error by 30–60% over the naïve version.

01
Forced choice over Likert

"Pick one of these two" outperforms 1–7 ratings on every metric. Eliminates acquiescence bias, scale-use bias, and decision fatigue. Calibrate the two options to be equally attractive on average.

02
Behavior, not attitude

"How often do you hike?" beats "Do you like hiking?". Past behavior is the single best predictor of future behavior. Attitudes are aspirational and slow to update; behaviors are honest and recent.

03
Images carry signal text can't

"Pick your perfect Saturday" with four photos extracts more variance than 10 minutes of typed answers. Vision bypasses verbal defenses and surfaces affective response.

04
Generate yeses, not noes

Self-perception theory: positive commitments shape identity, refusals don't. Frame every question as "which of these is most you" rather than "do you do X".

05
Anchor with the first question

The first real question sets the mental schema for everything after. Lead with an identity-anchor ("what kind of trip is this?") not a logistical detail. Logistics fold cleanly into a primed schema; the reverse does not.

06
Skip with defaults, never with blanks

Status-quo bias means a sensible default beats an empty field every time. Every skip should record an inferred default and a "we'll ask later" flag, not a null.

Demographics, full Big-Five batteries, and personality quizzes all underperform their friction cost. They feel research-grade but produce worse personalization than four well-chosen forced-choice scenarios. Dietary/accessibility belong at the activity-detail page (just-in-time), not in onboarding.

05 The v2 flow · 9 screens · 60–75 seconds

Ordering matters. We anchor with identity (step 02), capture logistics on the back of that schema, then layer in two psychometric forced-choice cards while the user is still warm. Permissions land last, when value has been established. Each step shows the exact ask, the dimension it scores on, the payload it produces, and why this wording over alternatives.

01

Welcome & sign-in

Identity ~5s
"Travel intentionally. Pick how you'd like to start."
Continue with Apple Continue with Google Continue with email

Why this wording: sign-in is the price of admission to a persistent product. Burying it behind "I have an account" leaks ~40% of returning users on new devices. The lead headline anchors brand voice (manifesto, not feature-list) and the three options keep the cognitive load at one tap.

Payloadauth: "apple", user_id: "abc-123", email: "…"
02

"What kind of trip is this?" — the anchor

Identity + motive ~6s
"What kind of trip is this?"
The adventure — push myself, see new terrain The crew bond — show up for people I love The reset — recover and slow down The discovery — learn something I didn't know

Why this wording: the four archetypes are drawn from travel-motivation research (novelty / relationship / escape-restore / self-development) but written in plain ACTZ voice. They double as anchor schema — every later question is interpreted through the chosen lens. Each archetype is calibrated to feel equally desirable; no objectively "right" answer.

Payloadtrip_motive: "adventure" | "crew" | "reset" | "discovery"
03

Where, when, from where

Logistics ~12s
"Where to?" · with origin + dates in one screen
Destination · 4 tiles or search From · auto-detected, one-tap confirm Dates · real picker · "± 3 days" flexibility toggle

Why this wording: the schema set by step 02 means "Where to?" is interpreted through the motive lens — adventurers see Maroon Bells, reset-seekers see Glenwood Springs. Origin and flexibility are the highest-leverage logistical inputs; both are pre-filled to keep the tap count low.

Payloaddest: "Aspen, CO", origin: "DEN", dates: ["2026-05-22","2026-05-27"], flex_days: 3
04

Travel mode + party size

Social orientation ~8s
"Who's with you?"
Solo · just me Crew · friends, partner, chosen family Family · with kids

Why this wording: "Who's with you?" is warmer than "travel mode" and avoids the corporate frame. The chosen mode reveals a follow-up: Solo asks "open to meeting other travelers?", Crew asks party size + decision style, Family asks party size + kid age buckets. Branching is OK because each branch is single-screen.

Payloadmode: "crew", party_size: 5, decisions: "vote"
05

"Pick your perfect Saturday" — forced-choice scenarios

Stimulation × hedonic ~8s
"Pick your perfect Saturday."
Photo · sunrise summit at 11,000ft (high stim · eudaimonic) Photo · long brunch with friends + a slow afternoon (low stim · hedonic) Photo · pottery class then an unfamiliar neighborhood walk (low stim · eudaimonic) Photo · pool day with a great playlist (low stim · hedonic)

Why this wording: the highest-signal single question in the entire flow. Each photo is calibrated to load on different points of the stimulation × hedonic plane. Forced choice between four equally appealing options bypasses social desirability — no one wants to look lazy on a Likert scale, but everyone has an honest favorite Saturday. Pick the photo set carefully so each visibly equates "great day."

Payloadstim_axis: 0.78, hedonic_axis: 0.42, scenario_pick: "summit"
06

Interests with two-tier intensity

Stimulation · preference ~15s
"What do you actually do?" — tap once for "open to", twice for "love"
Outdoor · Hiking, Camping, Cycling, Skiing Wellness & water · Hot springs, Water, Wildlife Culture · Food, Concerts, Photo, Hidden gems

Why this wording: the verb "do" — not "like" — anchors on behavior, not aspiration. The double-tap intensity gesture costs zero extra screen real estate and triples the ranking signal (Love = ×3 weight, Open-to = ×1, blank = 0). Cross-check against step 05's forced-choice answer for consistency.

Payloadinterests: { love: ["Hiking","Hot springs"], open: ["Photo","Food"] }
07

"Show me 3 sample days" — budget by revealed preference

Risk · financial ~10s
"Which of these sample days feels right for you?"
$98 · trailhead breakfast, ranger-led hike, taco truck dinner $210 · guided sunrise hike, lunch at the lodge, hot springs evening $420 · private guide, 3-course tasting menu, helicopter scenic flight "I'll figure it out as I go"

Why this wording: direct budget questions are actively lied on — people anchor low to feel frugal, then book up. Showing three calibrated example days defeats the lie because the user is picking a vibe, not declaring a number. The "I'll figure it out" escape hatch matters; without it, budget-anxious users abandon.

Payloadbudget_per_day: [180, 280], budget_signal: "comfort"
08

"Packed or open?" — planning style in one tap

Cognitive style ~5s
"On day one, would you rather…"
Have a packed itinerary lined up Have one anchor activity and the rest open Wing it entirely

Why this wording: the maximizer / satisficer distinction is otherwise impossible to capture in a single tap. This wording lets the AI either draft a 6-block day or surface one activity and a list of nearby options. Critical for not pissing off either group — packed itineraries terrify wanderers; open days frustrate planners.

Payloadplan_density: "anchor" | "packed" | "wing"
09

Permissions + identity commitment

Channel · identity ~10s
"One more thing — you're the kind of traveler who…"
"…shows up prepared and on time." "…travels with intention, not on autopilot." "…books the trip, then lives it." "…says yes to one weird thing per trip."

Why this wording: the identity-commitment question is the last act, after the user has earned a draft itinerary. Each statement is a positive self-perception cue (Bem) that bends later behavior toward the chosen identity — measurable in 90-day retention and referral. Below it: granular notification toggles + location permission, paired and framed as "so ACTZ can actually do the things we promised."

Payloadidentity_anchor: "intention", notif: { polls: true, bookings: true, marketing: false }, loc_grant: true

06 What not to ask — and why

A non-trivial chunk of onboarding design is restraint. These are the asks that feel personalizing but quietly tank completion rates and rarely move the model.

Long personality quizzes

Even a 10-item TIPI feels like homework on mobile. Four well-chosen forced-choice scenarios beat a full Big-Five battery on actual itinerary-fit metrics, at one-fifth the friction.

Drops completion ~25%, lifts model fit ~3%.
"What's your traveler archetype?"

BuzzFeed-style branded quizzes feel fun but score badly on retention — they prime users to see the result as a curiosity, not an identity. Use the archetype frame in step 02 without making it a quiz.

Cute, not predictive.
Direct demographic triplet

Age + gender + occupation feel like a phone screening. None of the three add predictive value beyond what archetype + budget + party-size already capture, and the privacy cost is real.

High friction, low signal, mild creepy.
Open-text "tell us about your dream trip"

Free text is the highest-friction input on mobile. Answers are vague, repetitive, and the AI can't act on them in onboarding. Save for a post-onboarding "make a wishlist" feature.

A textarea on screen 3 of 9 is an abandonment bomb.
Dietary & accessibility upfront

Asking about restrictions before the user has seen any value feels clinical. Better placed at the activity-detail page where it directly filters the next choice — same data, far better moment.

Right info, wrong moment.
Marketing-consent checkbox

The newsletter ask doesn't belong in onboarding. Handle it on first booking or in settings; bundling it now drops completion and conflates value-exchange with broadcast.

Tax the channel, not the welcome.

07 Bias mitigation

Eight biases that quietly corrupt onboarding data. The fixes are well known but rarely applied.

Social desirability survey methodology
People answer how they want to be seen, not how they are. "Are you adventurous?" returns ~80% yes.
Fix: never ask self-rating on traits. Use behavioral proxies and image-led scenarios that don't have a "right answer".
Acquiescence bias "yea-saying"
Respondents agree more than disagree by default, especially under time pressure.
Fix: avoid yes/no toggles for important measures. Forced choice between two positives.
Scale-use bias styles vary
Some users hug the middle of every Likert, others use the extremes. Cross-user comparison breaks.
Fix: avoid Likert. Forced choice and image picks have no per-user scale offset.
Order effects primacy / recency
First option in a list gets picked more often on desktop, last on mobile — both more than chance.
Fix: randomize option order per session for the four archetype cards and the Saturday-scenario set.
Anchoring first number sticks
A "Most users spend $X" hint biases all subsequent budget answers toward $X.
Fix: never show a benchmark number in a budget ask. Use three calibrated sample days, equally spaced.
Status quo bias default rules
Whatever is pre-selected gets accepted at high rates, even when it's wrong for the user.
Fix: use this for us on permissions defaulted off, against us by never pre-selecting a budget or motive.
Choice overload Schwartz
More than ~5 options per screen drops completion sharply. Decision fatigue compounds across screens.
Fix: 3–4 options per ask. Group interests into 3 visual clusters instead of one long list.
Sunk-cost bias why people skip
After ~3 screens, users want to finish, not abandon. They start tapping defaults.
Fix: put the highest-signal questions (steps 02, 05, 07) early. Permissions and identity-anchor go last, when they're tapping fastest.
